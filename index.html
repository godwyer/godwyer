Map 01

Projections of the Earth

This basic, four-part map looks at different projections of the globe to demonstrate how our approach can reshape space.

The four maps – Robinson, Goode Homolosine, Mercator, and Polyconic – offer 4 radically different ways of seeing the world. Being able to compare them all at once gives the viewer the ability to see how choices of projection can impact apparent size, distance, and familiarity with the geography being represented. The Mercator and Robinson maps are likely the most familiar and visibly pleasing of any of these projections for most viewers. However, even between these we can see how the distortion created by the Mercator projection overemphasizes the scale of northern and far southern land masses in ways that severely compromise utility and, it is argued, reinforce Western Imperialist perspectives. Importantly, the poles cannot be illustrated using a Mercator projection.

Comparing these we see that each has specific utility for some analyses and not for other, that each works with certain types of data and not with others, and that each is more useful in certain regions than in others. The outcomes of measurements from these different projections could be expected to vary in important ways which demonstrates the importance of projections when creating maps and performing geospatial research.

Skills Developed: • How to change the coordinate system used for a given map • How to define a projection for an undefined shapefile • How to create a new shapefile with a different projection

Map 02

First Chloropleth

Here we began to explore Chloropleth maps and use them to represent data.

Successful cartography is about creating visually engaging representations of data that not only convey information but attempt to do so ethically and accurately. We started our work on Chloropleth maps by representing the state-level population of Hispanic communities in the continental United States. In creating the color schemes we saw how the threshhold criteria for categories can impact the story that the map tells, how the color scheme can imbue emotional - even moral - content, and the importance of design choices on the final outcome which ultimately occurs not on the screen or page, but inside the viewer.

Skills Learned: • Using Census Data • Using the Symbology function • Methods and Classes in a graduated map • Map design basics

Software: ArcView Year: 2025
Map 03

Fresh Food, Multi-Modal Transit

By week 9 we had built our skills to perform more advanced cartographic analysis like identifying ideal sites for new healthy food options given proximity to transit.

The goal of this assignment was to identify potential opportunity zone sites for a new fresh food location given proximity to transit and to stores already involved in a City healthy food program. My recommendation was to give strong consideration to the north, just west of Broad Street. I based my recommendation on the density of transit options available that we don’t see in the other potential candidate geographies. It has near access to both regional rail and subway lines. While it wasn’t essential to the analysis, I also overlayed the final map with SEPTA bus routes to demonstrate the broader context of transit access. In this area we see both north-south and east-west bus routes to compliment the subway and regional rail stations nearby.

Skills Learned: • Spatial Joins • Erasing • Union • Intersects • Dissolving

Software: ArcView Year: 2025
